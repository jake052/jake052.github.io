*Final-year consultancy project · [Falmouth University](https://www.falmouth.ac.uk/) · May 2026*

Around [5% of adults with learning disabilities](https://www.mencap.org.uk/learning-disability-explained/research-and-statistics/employment-research-and-statistics) in the UK are in paid employment. The general working-age population sits at roughly 80%.

{{< figure src="/images/projects/trevince-employment-gap.png" alt="The employment gap: 5.1% of adults with learning disabilities in paid employment vs approximately 80% of the general working-age population" caption="Data: NHS England 2024; Mencap 2025" >}}

The gap is well documented. What's less visible is the mechanism that produces it: at eighteen, coordinated support largely disappears. The transition from children's to adult services is abrupt and poorly joined-up. [Nearly half of all NEET young people](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/sn06705/) in the UK are disabled — up from a quarter a decade ago.

{{< figure src="/images/projects/trevince-transition-cliff.png" alt="The transition cliff edge: structured support drops away at 18, with 957,000 young people NEET and 45.8% of them disabled" caption="Data: Department for Work and Pensions 2025; House of Commons Library 2026" >}}

{{< annotation >}}The system doesn't fail suddenly. It just quietly stops being a system.{{< /annotation >}}

[Trevince Estate](https://trevince.co.uk/) is an 800-year-old heritage estate in Cornwall, run by a three-person team across hospitality, horticulture, residential lettings, and renewable energy. The owners had identified community education as a future direction. The estate's brief was broad — future development across multiple strands. I chose to focus on the question my background equipped me to investigate: could inclusive, land-based provision for young adults with learning disabilities fit within the estate's existing operations — without overwhelming a team already stretched thin?

This was a live consultancy brief — my final-year entrepreneurship project at Falmouth. Not a case study, not a simulation. Real owners who'd have to live with whatever I recommended.

## How I approached it

Three evidence strands, each designed to answer a different question the owners needed answered before committing anything.

I mapped Cornwall's provision landscape — thirteen providers assessed against three criteria: learning disability-specific, land-based, and employability-focused. This wasn't a literature review exercise. It was figuring out whether Trevince would be duplicating something that already existed or filling a genuine gap.

I visited a working care farm near Camborne running exactly this kind of programme. What struck me wasn't just the operational model — it was the resourcefulness. Every activity fed the next: apple juice from the orchard, preserves from the kitchen, retail from the shop, placements from the employer network. And the pride. Everyone I spoke to — staff, learners — was eager to talk about what they did and why it mattered.

{{< annotation >}}The head of education's advice was five words: "Be somewhere people want to work."{{< /annotation >}}

I walked the estate itself, mapping where programme delivery could sit alongside existing operations — which zones were already separated from visitors, where safeguarding boundaries fell naturally, where the physical constraints were real versus manageable. My background in support work shaped what I noticed. Accessibility isn't a checklist you apply to a site map. It's individual, person by person. An 800-year-old working estate will never be clinical — but the evidence says that's actually the point. People in these programmes consistently report valuing settings that feel real rather than institutional.

Alongside the research, I coordinated a group of five second-year students preparing the implementation presentation — timelines, dependencies, delivery planning. My job was keeping the strategic research and the operational detail pointed at the same question. Having a team that engaged made that considerably easier than it could have been.

## What I recommended

A staged feasibility pathway — three phases, each building the evidence and confidence the next one requires. The design principle: start from what the estate already has, commit only what it can absorb, and let any stage stop without material loss.

The first stage would cost the estate nothing — becoming a host employer for supported placements through an existing provider, gaining productive labour while testing whether inclusive provision works on the ground, not just on paper. If that works, a second stage could formalise the partnership: scheduled programme days, a use-of-space fee, structured activity. A third stage — further down the line and only if demand warranted it — could integrate accessible infrastructure into a barn conversion the owners were already considering, part-funded through heritage capital grants covering up to 75% of project cost. The estate's exposure would be the match-funding residual, with indicative payback within five years.

Each stage has defined conditions before escalation. If the first doesn't work, the estate has lost nothing. If the second stalls, it's still generated income and community benefit. The pathway was designed so that stopping is always safe.

{{< annotation >}}The hardest part of consultancy is being honest about what the evidence doesn't support.{{< /annotation >}}

## What this taught me

I went into this with a declared position — I believed meaningful employment for people with learning disabilities was structurally under-served. The statistics shocked me more than I expected. The cliff edge at eighteen is so apparent, so obvious, that you'd think someone would have fixed it by now.

From my experience as a support worker, I knew that helping people find work where they're respected as individuals — not managed as cases — is hard. This project forced me to sit with the gap between what I wanted to recommend and what the evidence actually supported. A recommendation designed around the client's real constraints is harder to produce than one designed around what looks impressive. But it's the one that gets used.

That was the only measure I cared about.
