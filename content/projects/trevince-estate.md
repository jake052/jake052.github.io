*Live consultancy project · [Falmouth University](https://www.falmouth.ac.uk/) · May 2026*

Around [5% of adults with learning disabilities](https://www.mencap.org.uk/learning-disability-explained/research-and-statistics/employment-research-and-statistics) in the UK are in paid employment. The general working-age population sits at roughly 80%.

{{< figure src="/images/projects/trevince-employment-gap.png" alt="The employment gap: 5.1% of adults with learning disabilities in paid employment vs approximately 80% of the general working-age population" caption="Data: NHS England 2024; Mencap 2025" >}}

The mechanism behind that gap is mostly invisible: at eighteen, coordinated support quietly stops. The transition from children's to adult services is abrupt and poorly joined-up, and [nearly half of all NEET young people](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/sn06705/) in the UK are disabled — up from a quarter a decade ago.

{{< annotation >}}The system doesn't fail suddenly. It just quietly stops being a system.{{< /annotation >}}

[Trevince Estate](https://trevince.co.uk/) is an 800-year-old heritage estate in Cornwall, run by a three-person team. The owners had identified community education as a future direction. I focused on the question my background equipped me to investigate: could inclusive, land-based provision for young adults with learning disabilities fit within the estate's existing operations — without overwhelming a team already stretched thin?

This wasn't a case study or a simulation. Real owners who'd have to live with whatever I recommended.

## How I approached it

I mapped Cornwall's provision landscape — thirteen providers — to establish whether Trevince would be duplicating something that already existed or filling a genuine gap.

I visited a working care farm near Camborne running exactly this kind of programme. What struck me was the resourcefulness — apple juice from the orchard, preserves from the kitchen, placements from the employer network — and the pride. Everyone there wanted to talk about what they did and why it mattered.

{{< annotation >}}The head of education's advice was five words: "Be somewhere people want to work."{{< /annotation >}}

Then I walked the estate itself, mapping where a programme could sit alongside existing operations and where safeguarding boundaries fell naturally. An 800-year-old working estate will never be clinical. The evidence says that's the point: people in these programmes consistently value settings that feel real rather than institutional.

## What I recommended

A staged pathway. Start from what the estate already has, commit only what it can absorb, and make sure any stage can stop without loss.

Stage one costs the estate nothing: host supported placements through an existing provider, and find out whether inclusive provision works on the ground, not just on paper. If it does, stage two formalises the partnership — scheduled programme days, a use-of-space fee. Stage three, only if demand warrants it, builds accessible infrastructure into a barn conversion the owners were already considering, part-funded through heritage grants covering up to 75% of project cost. If stage one doesn't work, the estate has lost nothing. Stopping is always safe.

{{< annotation >}}The hardest part of consultancy is being honest about what the evidence doesn't support.{{< /annotation >}}

## What it taught me

I went in believing meaningful employment for people with learning disabilities was structurally under-served. The statistics still shocked me. The cliff edge at eighteen is so obvious you'd think someone would have fixed it by now.

This project forced me to sit with the gap between what I wanted to recommend and what the evidence actually supported. A recommendation designed around the client's real constraints is harder to produce than one designed around what looks impressive. But it's the one that gets used — and that was the only measure I cared about.
