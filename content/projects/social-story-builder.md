*The first People Helping People hypothesis · Spring 2026*

Social stories are short, visual, personalised documents that help people with learning disabilities and autism prepare for situations — a hospital visit, a new routine, a change of staff. They work. They're also slow to make, inconsistent in quality, and usually need a specialist. A support worker who knows exactly what someone needs can still spend hours producing one — or not produce it at all, because the shift doesn't have hours spare.

My first hypothesis was that this was the problem: the *outputs* of care are bad. Support plans are hard to read, visual tools take too long to make. Fix the end product.

## What I built

A working generator: structured personal data in — how someone communicates, what they need to prepare for, what matters to them — and a bespoke social story out, in minutes instead of hours. Not a mock-up. It does what it's supposed to do — you can [try it yourself](https://stories.peoplehelpingpeople.co.uk).

## What the market said

Then I tested the hypothesis against the evidence, and two things came back.

Six AI-first social story generators already existed — I'd be entering contested space against funded teams. And when I analysed twenty-seven local authority technology strategies, the sector was already pouring money into exactly this layer: better outputs, better dashboards, better visualisations. Crowded from both ends.

{{< annotation >}}The hypothesis was wrong, and the evidence said so clearly. That's the system working.{{< /annotation >}}

## Why this wasn't a failure

Eric Ries calls this validated learning: you build the smallest thing that tests the assumption, and sometimes the answer changes the question. Building the generator wasn't wasted either. Cohen and Levinthal's idea of absorptive capacity says your ability to recognise a new opportunity depends on what you've already built — and this build gave me the technical capability and the domain understanding to attempt something more ambitious.

It also forced the better question. If the output layer is crowded and still failing people, maybe the problem was never the documents. Maybe it's the fragmented data underneath them — the knowledge that exists in workers' heads and daily notes but never reaches the page.

That question became the [second hypothesis](/projects/care-plan-update-system/), and eventually the whole direction of [People Helping People](/projects/people-helping-people/).
