*A documented practice · Leadership research, 2026*

Most people use AI to produce things. I wanted to know what happens when you use it to interrogate yourself.

{{< figure src="/images/projects/reflective-gauge.png" alt="A hand-drawn gauge with the needle between outcome-dominant and relationally attentive leadership" caption="The dial I was trying to read." >}}

For a leadership research project, I put five real episodes from my own management history — two from retail, three from social care — through structured reflective interviews. The interviewer was an AI, given a strict brief: work through Gibbs' reflective cycle stage by stage, never evaluate, never praise, never reassure, and refuse to accept surface-level answers. Spoken interviews, not written — talking is harder to edit than typing, and the unguarded version is the useful one. The AI's only job was to ask the next question that pushed my thinking further.

## What it surfaced

The honest answer: things I had successfully not looked at for years.

One episode involved a staff member I'd promised to move out of a role he hated, and didn't. Pressed on causal reasoning — not what happened, but why — I heard myself say it: *"I think that affected how much effort I actually put into following through on my promise."* The "that" was personal dislike. I'd carried the story of a reasonable operational decision for years; the structured questioning located the bias underneath it in about fifteen minutes.

Another episode looked, from the outside, like textbook servant leadership: arriving 30–45 minutes early, unpaid, to set up the store and have coffee ready for the 5am team. Pressed on motivation: *"I'd love to think it was all altruistic, but it wasn't."* The routine reduced my own stress and helped me hit the targets my bonus depended on. Same behaviour, different leader than the one I'd believed I was.

{{< annotation >}}Feeling like a good leader and being one are not always the same thing — and the gap isn't visible without someone relentlessly asking why.{{< /annotation >}}

The method matters more than the technology. Reflective practice — Schön, Gibbs, Kolb — has always been foundational to good care leadership, and it has always had a supply problem: skilled supervision is scarce, peers are busy, and reflecting alone mostly means rehearsing the story you already tell. A well-briefed AI lowers that barrier. It's always available, it isn't bored, it has no stake in your self-image, and — properly instructed — it won't tell you what you want to hear.

## The honest limitations

This is a supplement, not a substitute — for supervision, peer support, or lived experience. And the failure modes are real. Retrospective reflection runs on memory, with all its self-serving edits. One of my most uncomfortable findings — that a "pastoral" suggestion to a struggling staff member was partly about moving a problem on — didn't surface in the interview at all. It only emerged later, during writing. A single session doesn't always disturb the story you tell yourself; sometimes it just helps you tell it more fluently. That's the line between reflection and rationalisation, and an agreeable AI can put you on the wrong side of it.

Which is why the brief prohibited evaluation and praise. At one point the AI tried to summarise my reflections back to me, neatly packaged. I stopped it — *"no, no, no, don't do this"* — because the moment it starts concluding on your behalf, you're consuming reflection rather than doing it.

## The bigger point

This is the same thesis as [People Helping People](/projects/people-helping-people/), applied to myself instead of a care record: AI in social care isn't there to replace human judgement. It's there to make the human judgement better — by surfacing what the human, left alone, reliably skips.
