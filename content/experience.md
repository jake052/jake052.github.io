## The short version

Retail taught me to run things. Support work taught me why running things isn't enough. Managing a care service taught me what the systems get wrong, and the degree gave me the tools to do something about it. Each step looks like a swerve until you see where it was heading.

## The longer version

### Retail management

My first management roles were in food retail — by my early twenties I was running a Greggs in Nottingham through COVID, and opened a new store that beat its launch targets by 180% with 20% year-on-year growth after that. Retail taught me operations, team leadership, and commercial thinking under pressure. It also taught me what KPI-driven environments do to how people lead — a lesson I didn't fully understand until much later.

### Frontline support work

Support work was my way into adult social care: working directly with people with learning disabilities and autism, day in, day out. This is where the motivation crystallised. You learn quickly that the person in front of you is rarely the problem — the systems around them usually are. You also learn exactly how care knowledge gets produced: in shift notes written at the end of a long day, in handover conversations, in the heads of workers who eventually leave.

### Service management — FitzRoy

At FitzRoy I was Deputy Service Manager launching a 12-bed, multi-million-pound supported living service from scratch: 650+ support hours a week, a 30+ staff team I recruited and kept below 10% turnover, regulatory compliance, an operating surplus, and a steering group with local authority and NHS partners on a new service model. I led complex transitions, including forensic and children's-to-adult placements — the exact cliff edge where coordinated support tends to vanish.

I've been the person trying to write a care plan from three contradictory assessments at 2am. That sentence is most of my CV.

### The deliberate step back

In 2023 I left management to study Business, Entrepreneurship & Innovation at Falmouth — graduating with First Class Honours and a final-year average of 84%. Running a retail store at twenty-one and a care team at twenty-three meant making decisions without the conceptual tools to understand what was going wrong. Stepping back to build those tools was the point: practice without understanding produces patterns that repeat.

{{< annotation >}}It looked like a step down. It was a run-up.{{< /annotation >}}

### Now: building at the intersection

I still do support work — at Brandon Trust, and with Go Beyond supporting adults with learning disabilities on holidays abroad — because staying connected to practice is where the real product insight comes from. Alongside it I'm building [People Helping People](/projects/people-helping-people/), a data fusion venture for social care, and planning postgraduate study in health data science.
