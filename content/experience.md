## The short version

I've spent most of my working life in social care — first as a support worker, then running teams, then wondering why the systems we used to help people were so consistently bad at it. That question turned into a degree, which turned into a dissertation, which turned into whatever this becomes next.

## The longer version

### Support work & care management

I started as a support worker in adult social care, working directly with people with complex needs. Over several years I moved into team leadership and service coordination — managing staff, overseeing care plans, navigating the gap between what people needed and what the system was set up to provide.

What I took from this: a detailed, ground-level understanding of how care data is produced, where it breaks down, and why the people who need it most are usually the last to see it. This isn't abstract for me — I've been the person trying to write a care plan from three contradictory assessments at 2am.

### Business Innovation (First Class Honours)

My undergraduate degree in Business Innovation gave me a framework for thinking about systems, organisations, and how things change (or don't). Final year average: 84%.

The degree was deliberately broad — strategy, innovation management, organisational behaviour — which turned out to be useful preparation for the interdisciplinary work I'm doing now.

### MSc Data Science & Modelling

Currently finishing at the University of Exeter. The MSc is where the care experience and the analytical training converge: my dissertation project ([People Helping People](/projects/people-helping-people/)) applies data fusion techniques to the fragmented care records problem I spent years experiencing firsthand.

Core areas: statistical modelling, machine learning, data engineering, and the ethics of linking sensitive personal data across organisational boundaries.
