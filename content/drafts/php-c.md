Care notes in. Existing plan in. Updated plan out — every change carrying a citation back to the note that justifies it, every suggestion approved by a human before it lands.

{{< figure src="/images/projects/php-living-plan.png" alt="The PHP prototype refreshing a care plan marked stale at 14 months, reading every shift note written since" caption="A plan 14 months stale, refreshed from every note written since. Try it: careplan.peoplehelpingpeople.co.uk" >}}

That's the working end of People Helping People — a data fusion layer for social care. The deeper idea: a person's reality is scattered across notes, assessments, incident reports and family conversations, and no one source tells the truth alone. Fuse them — the way defence fuses satellites and ground reports — and you get one living picture of a person instead of a folder of contradictions.

I'm building it because I spent eight years on the frontline and in service management watching the gap, and because my brother Ollie lives on the wrong side of it.

## What's proven, what's assumed, what's unknown

I keep this honest the same way I'd run any early venture — by labelling the claims.

**Proven.** The problem: 652 Safeguarding Adult Reviews in four years, three quarters citing poor information-sharing. The build: two working prototypes — [social stories](https://stories.peoplehelpingpeople.co.uk) and the [care plan updater](https://careplan.peoplehelpingpeople.co.uk) — running on synthetic data.

**Assumed.** That registered managers at small providers are the first buyer. That fused plans are measurably richer than manual ones. Credible, not yet confirmed.

**Unknown.** Pricing. And the hardest question in the project: this is AI mediating the voice of people who often can't verify what's written about them. That gets co-designed with the people the system describes — it's the product, not a compliance step.

## Why the gap exists

Eighty per cent of providers are on digital records, so the raw material is already being captured. The whole care-tech market sells outputs — forms, dashboards, documents. The layer underneath, the one that would make every output better, is empty. I'm not avoiding competition. I'm competing on a different axis.
