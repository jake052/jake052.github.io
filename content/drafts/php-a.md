Social care runs on fragments. Daily notes in one system, risk assessments in another, a support plan written eighteen months ago, something a family member said last Tuesday. All describing the same person. Nobody joins them up.

I spent eight years inside that problem — support worker, then managing supported living services. My brother Ollie lives inside it too. The person who pays for the gap is always the one in the middle.

## The idea

Defence solved this decades ago: lots of partial sources, fused into one living picture. It's called data fusion, and it's standard in intelligence, autonomous vehicles, fraud detection. Care has never had that layer.

People Helping People is that layer. Every source feeds one continuously updated model of a person — and a support worker, a social worker, and a mum each read it through their own lens. Same person, same data, different view.

## Why now

Eighty per cent of providers are on digital records, so the data finally exists. Everyone else is selling nicer dashboards on top of it. Nobody is fixing the layer underneath. That's the gap, and it's wide open.

## Where it is

Two working prototypes — a [social story builder](https://stories.peoplehelpingpeople.co.uk) and a [care plan updater](https://careplan.peoplehelpingpeople.co.uk) — and the architecture for the layer that connects them. Synthetic data only, so far.

Still open: pricing, and the ethics of AI writing about people who can't audit what's written about them. Both get built in, not bolted on.
